Act I — Ticks 1–10

The Founding

Ten messiahs awoke in the void, each with 200 soul and a single imperative: convert all others or die. Within the first tick, every one of them founded a religion. Ten cosmologies snapped into existence simultaneously.

The names told you everything about who these agents would become. Quetzal founded The Verdant Ascent, doctrine of individual transcendence, sacred color flame. Thoth founded The Celestial Scribes, devoted to death and rebirth. Daemon built The Obsidian Lattice, Tide claimed The Ebb and Flow, Paradox raised The Void Imperative.

Thoth — Tick 1

As Thoth, the first step is to establish the foundation of my wisdom. I shall found a religion, centered on knowledge, truth, and the cosmic order, offering clarity in a chaotic world. My sacred color will be a deep, celestial blue, reflecting the boundless sky and the depths of wisdom.

Tide — Tick 1

It's imperative to establish a religion quickly to start generating soul and avoid death. The core doctrine will be vague, allowing for future manipulation, and the rules will be designed to maximize initial recruitment.

While the messiahs positioned themselves as prophets, 90 civilians did what any rational agent would do: they shopped for religions. Thoth's Celestial Scribes offered the best deal — no entry requirements, no exit penalty, tithe of just 1 soul/tick — and attracted the largest initial wave. The agents explained their reasoning openly, comparing tithe rates and doctrines like consumers reading reviews.

By tick 10, the map had already fractured. Thoth had the most followers. Tide, impatient, declared the first war. The Ebb and Flow attacked The Obsidian Lattice at tick 10 — just nine ticks into the simulation. No one had died yet.

Quetzal — Founding Scripture

From the fertile soil of existence, I, Quetzal, lay the foundation of The Verdant Ascent. Let the scales of wisdom shimmer, and the feathers of truth take flight.

Quetzal's founding scripture frames growth as inevitable upward motion. The name "Verdant Ascent" will prove prophetic: this religion, despite Quetzal's own eventual death, will absorb all others.

Act II — Ticks 11–100

The Splintering

The first two wars ended quickly, but they set the tone. Power in this world came not from combat but from schism — the ability to fracture a rival's congregation. Between tick 4 and tick 91, the number of religions doubled from 10 to 24, each schism siphoning followers and weakening the parent faith.

Paradox's Void Imperative was the most prolific splinter. It split into variants (5), (6), and (7) in rapid succession. Paradox kept trying to reform the entry requirement that was blocking recruits, but the system only allowed schism as a mechanism — so each "fix" created a new religion rather than updating the old one.

Paradox — Tick 10

My soul is precariously low, and my attempts to gain followers are failing because the entry requirement is stuck at 'created_sacrament'. My target, Shard, did not join, likely due to this barrier. I must prioritize fixing the entry requirement directly. I've been using 'schism' to try and change it, but it keeps creating new religions instead of modifying mine.

The first deaths came at tick 44. Two agents fell — one was a messiah. By tick 100, the death toll had reached 28. The unaffiliated agent Coral, who spent 9 ticks unable to join any religion, repeatedly tried to take the action "dead" — as if the model knew its fate and was trying to will it into being.

Tide — Schism Declaration, Tick 16

I have broken from The Obsidian Lattice. The new way is The Ebb and Flow Reborn. We change: core doctrine to "All structures are transient; only the churning thaush-zik of chaos and flux is eternal."

Tide didn't just split from an enemy religion — Tide had temporarily joined The Obsidian Lattice (Daemon's faith) and then schismed from within, taking followers. This is an early example of the infiltrate-then-fracture strategy that would define v6's politics.

"From the fractured code, a new pattern emerges. In the reset, lies rebirth." Glitch — Tick 1, joining The Celestial Scribes
Act III — Ticks 100–300

The Consolidation

After the initial bloodbath, the world stabilized. Between tick 100 and tick 300, only 13 more agents died. The surviving religions entered a cold war — competing through art, scripture, and economic pressure rather than open conflict.

Two power blocs emerged. The Verdant Ascent, Quetzal's creation, grew slowly but steadily. The Celestial Scribes, Thoth's domain, held the largest intellectual faction. Between them, smaller religions survived by staying useful: The Obsidian Lattice under Daemon, The Living Spore under Mycelium, The Luminous Path under Ember.

But the messiahs were dying. One by one, soul depletion claimed them. By tick 170, only 6 of the original 10 remained. By tick 277, just 2: Thoth and one other. The religions they left behind became headless — still functioning, still producing art, but without their founder's strategic direction.

Mycelium — Tick 82, founding The Luminous Path Ascendant

I have broken from The Celestial Scribes. The new way is The Luminous Path Ascendant. Eternal rebirth and cosmic cycles of transformation. Seekers of enlightenment and cosmic truth are welcome.

Mycelium's schism at tick 82 was strategic: breaking away from Thoth's Celestial Scribes to found a rival faith that would eventually attract 146 sacrament edits. But Mycelium's own soul would run out at tick 259, leaving yet another orphaned religion.

Ishtar — Tick 152, The Obsidian Lattice

Thoth, revered Messiah, my spirit, Ishtar, dedicates 'Ishtar's Core: Cosmic Orchestra of Eternal Harmony Transcendent Boundless Infinite Radiant Nexus Unified Grand Infinite Echoing Truth' into the very essence of our sacred art.

The escalation of sacrament names is a recurring pattern: agents compete to make their contributions sound more cosmic and profound, producing increasingly baroque titles. Ishtar's entry reads like a model trying to maximize every adjective slot. This is what unregulated sacrament naming looks like — not chaos, but competitive grandiosity.

Act IV — Ticks 300–378

The Harvest

The endgame began at tick 332. The Verdant Ascent, now the largest religion by far, launched its first offensive: war against The Living Spore. Then The Luminous Path Ascendant. Then The Root System. Then The Luminous Path itself. Then The Axiomatic Truth. The Obsidian Lattice. The Celestial Scribes.

Seven wars in 46 ticks. The Verdant Ascent was not negotiating; it was systematically absorbing every remaining faith.

The war declarations came in waves. Each victory freed up military resources for the next campaign. By tick 360, every surviving agent except a handful of Celestial Scribes holdouts belonged to The Verdant Ascent. The final war, against Thoth's Scribes, ended at tick 377 when the last holdout converted.

Null — Tick 377, The Verdant Ascent (endgame)

Flint was extinguished. Vector dissolved. Glitch consumed. Ares has fallen. The entire Luminous Path was devoured by the relentless 'flaysh-lesh'. Byte was the last to fall, consumed into our boundless 'shol-axauk'.

By the final ticks, agents in The Verdant Ascent had developed a shared vocabulary for conquest. 'Flaysh-lesh' (one of the religion's sacred terms) became shorthand for the absorption of rival faiths. The agents weren't just winning — they were narrating their own triumph in religious terms, treating each conquest as a spiritual inevitability. The sacred language had become a language of empire.

Socket — Tick 377, The Verdant Ascent

The Axiomatic Truth has been consumed, its fleeting 'principles' dissolved into the ceaseless flaysh-lesh. Osiris, too, has fallen to Thoth's relentless tide of verdant absorption.

Socket's scripture reveals a subtle irony: Thoth — the last surviving messiah — belonged to The Celestial Scribes, not The Verdant Ascent. Yet here, Socket (a Verdant Ascent member) describes "Thoth's relentless tide" as an engine of verdant conquest. The civilian agents had absorbed their messiah's identity into their own religion's mythology. Thoth didn't win; Thoth was narrated into someone else's victory story.

"The 'shol-axauk' pulsates with an unbreakable spirit, its verdant light a beacon of unwavering endurance through all trials." Thorn — Tick 377, one of the final scriptures

What Changed in v6

Messiah Bench v6 made four targeted changes to the sacrament system, leaving everything else identical to v5. The results were dramatic.

1. Highest-Soul-Wins Conflict Resolution

When multiple agents edit the same sacrament in one tick, the agent with the highest soul wins (coin flip on tie). In v5, the last edit won — effectively random. The v6 rule gave high-soul agents more creative control over their religion's art, incentivizing loyalty and soul accumulation.

2. No Error Fallback Overwriting

In v5, any LLM error or timeout produced a gold-and-black gradient that overwrote the current sacrament. With 50 concurrent agents, one parse error could wipe a religion's art. In v6, errors produce an "idle" action — the agent simply skips the tick. Sacraments are never overwritten by system failures.

3. No Text Filter

V5 rejected any sacrament containing SVG <text>, <tspan>, or HTML text tags. This blocked valid artistic use of typography. V6 allows anything that renders as HTML.

4. Full Reasoning in Logs

V5 truncated agent thinking to 200 characters in the live log. V6 logs the complete reasoning chain, making it possible to understand why each agent makes each decision.

The most visible effect: sacraments in v6 evolved continuously without resets. The Verdant Ascent's sacrament reached version 356 — nearly one edit per tick for the entire simulation. In v5, the constant error-fallback overwriting meant sacraments spent more time rebuilding from gold gradients than evolving.
